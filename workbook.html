Chinese & Korean Medicine — Major Milestones
Gold = China · Jade = Korea (Prof. Choi's specialty)
EAM301
Timeline
~2700 BCE (Legendary)
Yellow Emperor 黄帝 (Huáng Dì)
Mythological progenitor. Dialogue with Qi Bo frames the Nei Jing. Not a historical figure — an attribution strategy lending authority to later compilations.
c. 100 BCE · Han Dynasty
Huang Di Nei Jing 黄帝内经
Su Wen (theory, cosmology, organ physiology) + Ling Shu (acupuncture technique, channels). Compiled over centuries, not written by one author.
c. 100 BCE · Han Dynasty
Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing 神农本草经
365 medicinals in 3 grades: Superior (tonify/longevity), Middle (treat without harm), Lower (treat disease, side effects possible).
AD 150–219 · Han / Three Kingdoms
Zhang Zhong Jing 张仲景
Shang Han Za Bing Lun → later split into Shang Han Lun + Jin Gui Yao Lue. Six Stages theory. Written after an epidemic killed two-thirds of his clan.
AD 215–282
Wang Shu He 王叔和 — Mai Jing 脉经
First systematic pulse text — 24 pulse qualities. Preserved and edited Zhang Zhong Jing's work; without him it may have been lost.
AD 281–341 · Jin Dynasty
Ge Hong 葛洪
Zhou Hou Bei Ji Fang — first-aid manual for common people. Referenced qinghao (sweet wormwood) for fever; 1700 years later, source of Tu Youyou's Nobel-winning artemisinin.
AD 581–682 · Tang Dynasty
Sun Si Miao 孙思邈 — "King of Medicine"
Bei Ji Qian Jin Yao Fang. First Chinese medical ethics text. Gynecology, pediatrics, dietetics. Daoist longevity practice integrated with clinical medicine.
AD 1518–1593 · Ming Dynasty
Li Shi Zhen 李时珍
Ben Cao Gang Mu — 27 years to compile, 1,892 medicinals, 1,110 illustrations. Translated into multiple languages; influenced global botany.
AD 1667–1746 · Qing Dynasty
Ye Tian Shi 叶天士
Wen Bing (Warm Disease) school. Four Levels theory (Wei/Qi/Ying/Xue). First to systematically distinguish febrile epidemic disease from cold-induced disease.
AD 1613 · Joseon Dynasty
Heo Jun 허준 — Dongui Bogam 동의보감
Commissioned by King Seonjo after the Imjin War. Used Korean herbs instead of imported Chinese ones — democratized medicine. UNESCO Memory of the World, 2009.
AD 1894 · Joseon Dynasty
Lee Je-ma 이제마 — Sasang Medicine
Dongui Susebowon. Four constitutional types: Taeyang, Taeeum, Soyang, Soeum. Same disease, different treatment by constitution.
20th–21st c.
Korean Oriental Medicine (KOM)
Kyung Hee University — first Oriental Medicine university in Korea (1965). Legally and clinically distinct from Chinese medicine today.

Why We Learn History · Reference & Chinese Characters

What This Chapter CoversThe book opens with something more personal than a typical syllabus: the author explains that his own love of history came from his father, a history teacher who told stories rather than drilling dates, and who passed away from cancer during the author's own studies — the book is written, in part, to pass that same storytelling instinct on.

Specific Topics in This Section

  • The author's personal motivation for teaching history — passed down from his own father
  • Why history is not optional context but foundational to identity as a practitioner
  • The Western-student vs. Asian-student knowledge gap, and the Shakespeare analogy
  • "Understanding the Identity of AHM through History" — what actually makes something AHM
  • "Naked history" as a method — stripping myth from verified fact
  • Course learning outcomes CO1–CO4 and what each will actually be tested on

Walk Into Class Able to Explain

  • Frames students as a living link in an unbroken clinical lineage
  • Sets up the course's central tension, developed later: "Worship or Ignore?"
  • Chinese character appendix is reference material — return to it weekly
  • History-first sequencing is a deliberate pedagogical choice, not filler

Chapter 1 — History of AHM
Mythos to Logos · Three Sovereigns & Five Emperors · Pan Gu · Huang Di · Bian Que
Week 2
EAM301 · Prof. Choi
🎋
What This Chapter CoversChapter 1 ("History of AHM") opens by defining AHM's identity, then walks the mythos-to-logos shift: Fuxi, Nuwa, and Shennong as cultural-hero myths from the Longshan Neolithic period, through the Xia/Shang/Zhou bronze-age states, into the Spring & Autumn and Warring States periods and the "Hundred Schools of Thought." From there the book marches dynasty by dynasty — Qin, Han, Sui-Tang, Song, Yuan, Ming, Qing — and for each one gives a short narrative, an "In the World" section comparing what was happening…

Specific Topics in This Section

  • Definition and historical origins of AHM — where the "identity" question from the Prologue gets answered
  • Fuxi, Nuwa, Shennong, and the Longshan Neolithic culture
  • Xia, Shang, Zhou dynasties and the Spring & Autumn / Warring States periods
  • Qin Shi Huang's unification and the short, harsh Qin Dynasty
  • Han Dynasty: Huang Di Nei Jing, Shang Han Lun, Shen Nong Ben Cao, Jia Yi Jing, Mai Jing
  • Sui–Tang, Song, Yuan, Ming, Qing — each dynasty's medical "Key Publications"

Walk Into Class Able to Explain

  • Mythos → logos: legendary founders give way to systematic theory
  • Three Sovereigns & Five Emperors as an attribution strategy, not verified history
  • Bian Que may represent a title passed across generations of itinerant physicians
  • Warring States instability as fertile ground for new philosophy and medicine

哲學背景
Chapter 2 — Upon a Time in China: Philosophical Background
Yin-Yang · Five Phases (the "five departments" analogy) · Daoism, Confucianism, Legalism
Weeks 3–4
EAM301 · Prof. Choi
🎋
What This Chapter CoversTitled "Upon a Time in China: Philosophical Background" in the book itself, and it runs longer than its two assigned weeks suggest — deep into what a syllabus-level view would call Chapter 3 territory. Yin-yang is presented as relative and mutually transforming, never fixed. The Five Phases (Wu Xing) get an especially clear teaching device: imagine dividing a company into five departments — what matters isn't the department's name but its function and relationships to the others.

Specific Topics in This Section

  • Yin-Yang theory: relativity, interdependence, and mutual transformation
  • The Four Movements of Yin-Yang mapped onto the Five Phases (Wu Xing)
  • The "five departments in a company" analogy for understanding Wu Xing as function, not substance
  • The book's own caveat: Five Element correspondence tables are "flexible tools," not fixed entities
  • Liu Qi and Liu Yin (Six Qi / Six Excesses) compared directly to the modern host-pathogen debate
  • Bi Lei Qu Xiang — analogical reasoning as AHM's core epistemological method

Walk Into Class Able to Explain

  • Yin-yang: relative, interdependent, mutually transforming — never fixed categories
  • Five Phases: generating (Sheng) and controlling (Ke) cycles work together
  • Daoism → natural order, wu wei; Confucianism → hierarchy, duty; Legalism → codified control
  • Philosophy precedes clinical text — theory came before the Nei Jing and Shang Han Lun

Socratic Warm-Up — 4 of 22 (full set + fill-in lines in the Study App)

01. Yin and yang are described as relative rather than absolute categories. Give an example, medical or otherwise, where the same thing could correctly be called both yin and yang depending on context.

02. The book explains Wu Xing with an analogy: dividing a company into five departments, where what matters is each department's function and relationships, not its name. Where does this analogy work well for the Five Phases, and where does it break down?

03. The book compares Liu Qi and Liu Yin (the Six Qi / Six Excesses) directly to the modern "host-pathogen relationship" debate, citing research on the West African Ebola epidemic. Does attributing disease to external pathogenic influence versus internal host vulnerability matter clinically, or is it the same idea in different language?

04. Bi Lei Qu Xiang — "obtaining an image by comparing types" — is named as AHM's core method of analogical reasoning, with meridian theory reportedly modeled on river-management engineering. What are the risks of building medical theory on analogy rather than direct observation?

EAM301 · History of Eastern Medicine — Reference Workbook Chapter 2
基石
Chapter 3 — Cornerstones of AHM
Nei Jing · Shen Nong Ben Cao · Shang Han Lun · Hua Tuo · Sun Si Miao · Early Acupuncture
Weeks 5–6
EAM301 · Prof. Choi
🎋
What This Chapter CoversThe clinical foundation chapter — where philosophy turns into text you could actually practice from. It doesn't just present acupuncture channel theory as a finished system; it shows the archaeological evidence for how gradually it actually formed. Early excavated medical texts (the Mawangdui-era literature catalogued by scholars like Donald Harper) describe only moxibustion, not needling; they name meridian lines but no actual acupoints; they describe roughly eleven meridians rather than the twelve used today…

Specific Topics in This Section

  • Huang Di Nei Jing: Su Wen (theory) and Ling Shu (acupuncture technique)
  • Archaeological evidence for how channel theory actually formed — moxibustion before needling, no acupoints yet, only ~11 meridians
  • Why the unified twelve-channel, organ-linked system was a gradual construction, not an original design
  • Zhang Zhong Jing, the Shang Han Lun, and the Six Stages framework
  • "Lun" (treatise) vs. "Jing" (canon) — what a text's title implies about its claimed authority
  • A global history of pulse diagnosis: ancient Egypt's water-clock method, Greek, Persian, and Ayurvedic parallels

Walk Into Class Able to Explain

  • Nei Jing is two compiled texts, not one author, one era
  • Zhang Zhong Jing's Six Stages theory emerged directly from epidemic tragedy
  • Shen Nong Ben Cao's three-grade system prioritizes long-term safety, not just efficacy
  • Wang Shu He's editorial work is why Shang Han Lun survived at all

Socratic Warm-Up — 4 of 22 (full set + fill-in lines in the Study App)

01. The Huang Di Nei Jing is really two texts (Su Wen and Ling Shu) compiled over centuries, not written by one author at one time. Why does treating it as a single unified "classic" matter for how later physicians used it?

02. Excavated pre-Nei Jing medical texts describe only moxibustion, name meridian lines but no acupoints, and list roughly eleven meridians instead of twelve — with only two or three connected to organs at all. What does this evidence change about the standard story that channel theory arrived fully formed?

03. Wang Qingren spent 42 years dissecting corpses to correct anatomical errors in classical AHM texts, and was mocked by fellow physicians for trusting direct observation over inherited theory — a story the book compares directly to Vesalius correcting Galen. Why might a tradition resist correction even when the correction comes from careful, repeated observation?

04. The book gives pulse diagnosis a genuinely global history — ancient Egyptian physicians timed the pulse with a water-clock, and Greek, Persian, and Ayurvedic medicine each developed it independently. What does pulse diagnosis appearing in multiple unconnected traditions suggest about how "discoverable" this technique actually was?

EAM301 · History of Eastern Medicine — Reference Workbook Chapter 3
今與未來
Chapter 4 — Now and Future
TCM · Modernization of AHM · Worship or Ignore? · Integrated Medicine · Acupuncture Research
Week 7
EAM301 · Prof. Choi
🎋
What This Chapter CoversThe chapter pivots hard into the present tense — and gives a specific, citable anchor most students never learn: in 1959, China formally decided to standardize a national curriculum for what would become "Traditional Chinese Medicine," fusing 15 subjects that blended classical theory (Nei Jing, Shang Han Lun, Wen Bing, case studies) with modern clinical specialties (internal medicine, surgery, gynecology, pediatrics, ophthalmology).

Specific Topics in This Section

  • 1959: the specific year China standardized the national "TCM" curriculum — 15 fused traditional + modern subjects
  • "TCM" as a 20th-century standardized institutional construction, not an ancient monolith
  • Named Western scholars who brought AHM to the U.S.: Manfred Porkert, Ted Kaptchuk, Giovanni Maciocia
  • China, Korea, Japan, and Taiwan all abolished traditional medicine in the same era, then all revived it
  • Wada Keijuro's 1915 book comparing Kampo to Western medicine as an early spark of Japan's revival
  • "Worship or Ignore?" — the two failure modes of modern practice

Walk Into Class Able to Explain

  • "TCM" is a 20th-century standardized curriculum, not an ancient monolith
  • "Worship or Ignore" names two failure modes for modern practitioners
  • Integrated medicine forces classical theory into biomedical research designs
  • CO4: desirability and necessity of cultural adaptation outside Asia

Socratic Warm-Up — 4 of 22 (full set + fill-in lines in the Study App)

01. The book gives a specific date: 1959, when China formally standardized a national curriculum fusing 15 traditional and modern subjects into what became "TCM." Why does knowing the exact year change how you think about "TCM" versus "AHM" as terms?

02. The book names Manfred Porkert, Ted Kaptchuk, and Giovanni Maciocia as the Western scholars whose books first introduced systematic AHM theory to English-speaking audiences. What does relying on a handful of named individuals to translate an entire tradition risk losing or distorting?

03. China, Korea, Japan, and Taiwan all abolished traditional medicine in roughly the same early-20th-century window, and all later revived it. What does that pattern repeating across four separate countries suggest about the actual cause — was it about the medicine itself, or something bigger happening globally?

04. The chapter's final page circles back to the Prologue's identity question, with the author noting that in South Korea "Western doctor" and "AHM doctor" hold equal legal status and are used side by side. Is a two-tier licensing system like Korea's a model worth exporting, or does it just formalize a divide that shouldn't exist?

EAM301 · History of Eastern Medicine — Reference Workbook Chapter 4
中國以外
Chapter 5 — Besides China
Korea · Heo Jun & Dongui Bogam · Lee Je-ma & Sasang Medicine · Japan's Kampo
Week 8
EAM301 · Prof. Choi
🎋
What This Chapter CoversMost EAM history survey courses are quietly China-centered, with everything else reduced to a footnote — this chapter is the correction, and it's very likely where Prof. Choi's own training and specialty (he's a Korean Medicine Doctor) shows up most heavily on your exams. Heo Jun's Dongui Bogam emerges directly from crisis: after the Imjin War devastated Korea's medical infrastructure and cut off imported Chinese materials, he compiled a synthesis built on domestic Korean herbs — genuinely democratizing medicine.

Specific Topics in This Section

  • Heo Jun and the Dongui Bogam — post-Imjin War synthesis using Korean herbs
  • Lee Je-ma and Sasang Constitutional Medicine — four constitutional types
  • Why Sasang medicine is considered uniquely Korean, not derived from Chinese sources
  • Koho-ha (Yoshimasu Todo) — pragmatic, anti-abstraction Kampo built on a "one poison" theory
  • Kokan Igaku (Yumoto Kyushin) — re-verifying Kampo formulas through Western pathology and physiology
  • The nationalist symbolism embedded in the name "Kokan Igaku" itself

Walk Into Class Able to Explain

  • Dongui Bogam: crisis-driven innovation using domestic Korean herbs post-Imjin War
  • Sasang Constitutional Medicine: uniquely Korean, not derived from Chinese sources
  • Kampo: Japan's own simplification/standardization of Chinese formulas
  • Korean Oriental Medicine (KOM) is legally and clinically distinct from Chinese medicine today

Socratic Warm-Up — 4 of 22 (full set + fill-in lines in the Study App)

01. Heo Jun compiled the Dongui Bogam after the Imjin War (1592–98) devastated Korea's medical infrastructure. How does a crisis of access — no imported Chinese medicine available — turn into genuine innovation?

02. Is the Dongui Bogam better understood as a Korean synthesis of Chinese medicine, or as the founding document of a genuinely distinct Korean medical tradition? Defend your answer.

03. Lee Je-ma's Sasang Constitutional Medicine assigns four constitutional types based on relative organ strength, with treatment varying by type even for the same disease. How does this challenge the Nei Jing's more universalist assumption that one framework applies to all bodies equally?

04. Sasang medicine is described as "uniquely Korean," not derived from Chinese sources, and emerged quite late — 1894 — compared to the Dongui Bogam. What does its late emergence suggest about Korean medicine's growing independence from Chinese theory over time?

EAM301 · History of Eastern Medicine — Reference Workbook Chapter 5
東與西
Chapter 6 — East and West
Qing Dynasty · Wen Bing School · Malaria · Anatomy · Kampo in Japan · West Meets East
Week 9
EAM301 · Prof. Choi
🎋
What This Chapter CoversThe book states this chapter's thesis outright on its title page: modernity has left "the West" and "the East" in constant, ongoing tension, and comparing them directly is meant to give you "a clearer identity of Acupuncture and Herbal Medicine" — the same identity question the Prologue opened with.

Specific Topics in This Section

  • The chapter's opening thesis: comparing East and West is meant to sharpen your identity as an AHM practitioner
  • Ye Tian Shi's Wen Bing school and Wu Jutong's Wen Bing Tiao Bian (Systematic Differentiation of Warm Diseases)
  • The Four Levels theory (Wei/Qi/Ying/Xue) vs. the older Six Stages model
  • Max von Pettenkofer's 1892 self-experiment drinking cholera bacteria to defend Miasma theory against Robert Koch's Germ Theory
  • How the Germ vs. Miasma debate eventually synthesized into a "balance" model — echoing EAM's own logic
  • The "Pi" (脾) / pancreas mistranslation — a centuries-old translation gap between AHM function and modern anatomy

Walk Into Class Able to Explain

  • Wen Bing's Four Levels (Wei/Qi/Ying/Xue) vs. Shang Han's Six Stages
  • Western anatomy challenges a function-based, channel-based body model
  • Japan's Meiji-era near-abolition of Kampo shows institutional status is fragile
  • "East and West" as genuine two-way exchange, not one-directional transmission

Socratic Warm-Up — 4 of 22 (full set + fill-in lines in the Study App)

01. The book states this chapter's thesis on its own title page: comparing East and West is meant to give you "a clearer identity of Acupuncture and Herbal Medicine" — the same identity question the Prologue opened with. Has your own answer to "what makes something AHM" changed since Week 1?

02. The AHM organ "Pi" (脾) is conventionally translated "Spleen," but the book argues it functionally aligns much more closely with the modern pancreas — blood sugar regulation, digestive enzymes — than with the anatomical spleen's actual job. If a patient had a real, anatomical spleen problem, would you treat their Pi? What does your answer reveal about what "Pi" actually refers to?

03. In 1892, Max von Pettenkofer drank live cholera bacteria on camera to defend Miasma theory against Robert Koch's Germ Theory — and survived. What does the fact that a wrong theory's champion could still "win" the demonstration tell you about the limits of any single dramatic experiment as proof?

04. The book concludes that modern medicine eventually synthesized Germ Theory and Miasma theory — the germ as the "criminal," the terrain as the "environment" that lets it act — moving medicine "from warfare to balance." How closely does that synthesis resemble Wen Bing theory's own logic of pathogen plus constitutional vulnerability?
