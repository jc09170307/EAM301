Part B — Short Answer: what a strong answer includes

These are starting points, not full model answers — these are Socratic discussion questions, so a complete answer should be in your own words and go further than the hint below.

1. Framing you as a "link in the chain" implies accountability for how faithfully and skillfully the tradition is carried forward — not just personal skill-building.
2. Naming EAM's journey into the U.S. positions this course, and your own training, as the tradition's next chapter, not a separate offshoot.
3. Transmission history includes real gaps and near-losses (e.g., Wang Shu He almost losing the Shang Han Lun) — "unbroken" is aspirational framing, not a literal claim.
4. Reverence keeps you humble and curious; uncritical acceptance stops you from asking "why" — which is exactly what a Socratic classroom demands.
5. A syllabus putting 90% of the grade on 2 exams and naming the Socratic method tells you VUIM values active, on-the-spot command of material over passive familiarity.

醫學史
🔑 Answer Key — Chapter 1 — History of AHM
Term definitions + short-answer model starting points
EAM301

Part A — Key Terms

Mythos to Logos — The historical shift from legend-based to systematic, reasoned medical explanation.
Three Sovereigns & Five Emperors — Legendary god-kings credited with founding civilization and medicine; not verified history.
Pan Gu — Cosmogonic creation figure whose myth establishes the cosmic order later medical theory assumes.
Huang Di (legendary) — The "Yellow Emperor" — a literary/attributional device giving authority to later compiled texts, not a verified person.
Bian Que — Semi-legendary physician, possibly a title used by multiple itinerant physicians across generations.
Warring States Period — Era of near-constant conflict (with the Spring & Autumn Period) that produced major philosophical and medical innovation.

Part B — Short Answer: what a strong answer includes

These are starting points, not full model answers — these are Socratic discussion questions, so a complete answer should be in your own words and go further than the hint below.

1. Creation myths establish the cosmic order everything else — including the body — is assumed to follow; medicine's origin story needs a universe to exist in first.
2. Constant war destabilizes existing authority structures, which is exactly when new philosophical alternatives, like Daoist balance and withdrawal, gain traction.
3. Divination and ritual gave early medicine cultural authority even before it had systematic theory — that authority was real, even if the theory came later.
4. You can discard the literal claim while preserving the legend as cultural memory and a shorthand for the tradition's values — most historians do exactly this.
5. Emphasize that separating myth from history is itself a modern historical skill worth learning, regardless of whether ancient Chinese medicine happens to be the subject.

哲學背景
🔑 Answer Key — Chapter 2 — Upon a Time in China: Philosophical Background
Term definitions + short-answer model starting points
EAM301

Part A — Key Terms

Yin-Yang — Paired, relative, mutually-transforming opposites — never fixed categories.
Five Phases (Wu Xing) — Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, Water — a correspondence system modeling relationships and change.
Sheng cycle — The Five Phase generating cycle (e.g., Wood feeds Fire).
Ke cycle — The Five Phase controlling/restraining cycle (e.g., Wood parts Earth).
Wu Wei — Daoist principle of non-forcing, effortless action aligned with natural order.
Legalism 法家 — Philosophy of codified law and centralized control; EAM's faintest, most indirect philosophical influence.

Part B — Short Answer: what a strong answer includes

These are starting points, not full model answers — these are Socratic discussion questions, so a complete answer should be in your own words and go further than the hint below.

1. Treating a patient as permanently "yin type" ignores how presentation shifts with season, time of day, and disease stage — you'd miss the moment treatment needs to change too.
2. A cyclical model lets you predict knock-on effects (a weak Kidney affecting the Liver, which affects the Spleen) that a straight-line cause-effect model would miss entirely.
3. The sun example works for a simple day/night cycle, but breaks down for anything without a clean periodic rhythm — which describes most of the body most of the time.
4. Both frameworks describe how change propagates through interconnected parts, but Five Phase relationships are fixed and symbolic while systems-biology feedback loops are measured and mechanistic.
5. Legalism usually gets the least classroom time because its influence on EAM is more indirect (standardization, state medicine) than the more visible Daoist and Confucian threads.

🔑 Answer Key — Chapter 3 — Cornerstones of AHM
Term definitions + short-answer model starting points
EAM301

Part A — Key Terms

Huang Di Nei Jing — The foundational classic, compiled over centuries — not a single author or era.
Su Wen — "Simple Questions" — the Nei Jing's theory and cosmology half.
Ling Shu — "Spiritual Pivot" — the Nei Jing's acupuncture technique and channel half.
Shang Han Lun — Zhang Zhong Jing's clinical text on cold-induced disease, written after a devastating epidemic.
Six Stages theory — Zhang Zhong Jing's staged framework for how cold-induced disease progresses through the body.
Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing — First materia medica; 365 medicinals organized into three toxicity/safety grades.

Part B — Short Answer: what a strong answer includes

These are starting points, not full model answers — these are Socratic discussion questions, so a complete answer should be in your own words and go further than the hint below.

1. Splitting theory from technique suggests the compilers saw understanding "why" and knowing "how" as two distinct, both-necessary skill sets — not one continuous lesson.
2. Surgery requires infrastructure (anesthesia, sterility, recovery care) that herbal medicine and acupuncture don't — without that infrastructure, the technique couldn't scale even if it worked.
3. A first-aid manual has to work without a trained reader present to interpret it, so it favors simple, single-symptom instructions over layered diagnostic reasoning.
4. Herbal classification (grades, toxicity) has probably changed least over the centuries; channel theory and clinical staging have likely been reinterpreted the most.
5. There's no single right answer here — build your case on which text later physicians actually had to master first, then let the counterargument show how little EAM would resemble itself without the other.

Part A — Key Terms

TCM (modern) — The standardized, government-organized curriculum built in the 20th century — not an ancient monolith.
Worship or Ignore? — The two failure modes for modern practitioners relating to this tradition's history.
Integrated medicine — Clinical settings combining EAM and biomedical approaches, requiring theory to fit biomedical research designs.
CO4 — Course outcome on the desirability and necessity of cultural adaptation outside Asia.
Modernization of AHM — 20th-century institutionalization: licensing, accredited programs, standardized terminology.
Acupuncture research — Modern clinical trials attempting to validate/explain EAM techniques in biomedical terms.

Part B — Short Answer: what a strong answer includes

These are starting points, not full model answers — these are Socratic discussion questions, so a complete answer should be in your own words and go further than the hint below.

1. "Worship" risks refusing anything that updates or challenges the tradition; "Ignore" risks practicing techniques as empty motions stripped of the reasoning that makes them coherent.
2. You can honor a patient's framework (results, not belief) while still practicing from your own trained understanding of why the technique works — those aren't mutually exclusive.
3. Licensing formalizes a baseline of competence across many practitioners at once, where master-apprentice transmission depended entirely on one teacher's judgment and availability.
4. Funding tends to flow toward mechanisms and outcomes that are easiest to measure with existing tools, which quietly shapes what gets called "evidence-based" regardless of what actually works.
5. Build your answer around whether earlier adaptations (into Korea, Japan) preserved the internal logic of the medicine, while today's adaptation risks replacing that logic with a different one entirely.

🔑 Answer Key — Chapter 5 — Besides China
Term definitions + short-answer model starting points
EAM301

Part A — Key Terms

Dongui Bogam — Heo Jun's 1613 synthesis using Korean herbs after the Imjin War; UNESCO Memory of the World, 2009.
Heo Jun — Author of the Dongui Bogam; often called "the Zhang Zhong Jing of Korea."
Sasang Constitutional Medicine — Lee Je-ma's system of four constitutional types requiring different treatment for the same disease.
Lee Je-ma — Author of the Dongui Susebowon (1894); considered a uniquely Korean theoretical contribution.
Kampo — Japan's own historical simplification and standardization of Chinese herbal formulas.
Korean Oriental Medicine (KOM) — Korea's modern, legally and clinically distinct medical system, institutionalized from 1965 (Kyung Hee University).

Part B — Short Answer: what a strong answer includes

These are starting points, not full model answers — these are Socratic discussion questions, so a complete answer should be in your own words and go further than the hint below.

1. Look at what changed (ingredients, accessibility, organization) versus what stayed the same (core theory, cosmology) to decide how much genuine independence the text represents.
2. Vietnam's tradition is often left out simply because most English-language EAM curricula were built around Chinese and, secondarily, Korean and Japanese sources — not because Đông y is less developed.
3. A chapter title admitting "everyone else" comes after China is itself evidence of how the field's historical narrative has traditionally been organized, and whose story gets centered.
4. International heritage recognition is as much a diplomatic and cultural-identity statement as a historical one — it signals how a nation wants its medical history seen abroad.
5. The honest answer is you'll need to actively seek out that training yourself, since it likely won't appear in a standard TCM-centered curriculum unless a program specifically teaches it.

🔑 Answer Key — Chapter 6 — East and West
Term definitions + short-answer model starting points
EAM301

Part A — Key Terms

Wen Bing school — Ye Tian Shi's Warm Disease school, distinguishing febrile epidemic disease from cold-induced disease.
Ye Tian Shi — Qing-dynasty physician who formulated Four Levels theory; studied under 17 teachers.
Four Levels (Wei/Qi/Ying/Xue) — Staged model tracking how warm/febrile disease penetrates progressively deeper into the body.
Qing Dynasty — The era of Wen Bing school innovation and early sustained contact with Western medicine.
Western anatomy — Dissection-based structural body model that challenged Chinese medicine's function-based channel theory.
Zang-Fu theory — Chinese organ theory describing function and relationship more than fixed anatomical structure.

Part B — Short Answer: what a strong answer includes

These are starting points, not full model answers — these are Socratic discussion questions, so a complete answer should be in your own words and go further than the hint below.

1. Both are staged, deepening-penetration models — the real difference is what triggers the progression (cold vs. warm pathogens) and therefore where in the sequence a physician intervenes.
2. A tradition's survival can depend more on political will and institutional funding than on its clinical track record — worth remembering before assuming today's boundaries are purely evidence-based.
3. Chinese medical theory prioritized function and relationship (how organs affect each other) over fixed structure, so detailed anatomy answered fewer of the questions the theory actually needed answered.
4. Argue based on which change proved more durable in actual clinical practice over the following century — internal refinements tend to get absorbed quietly; external challenges tend to force visible, lasting adaptation.
5. There's no single correct answer — build your case on which framework better matched the epidemiological pattern actually being observed, then acknowledge what the other approach would have caught that yours might miss.
