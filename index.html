🐲 Foundational Question Frameworks

"Why did this medical idea emerge at THIS historical moment? What social, political, or cultural conditions made it possible or necessary?"
→ 🏯 The Context Question — most common Socratic frame in EAM301
This is the core analytical move in medical history. For any development — a new text, a new technique, a new school of thought — ask: What dynasty? What political stability? Who had access to medical knowledge? What was the relationship between folk medicine and court medicine? Were there external influences (trade routes, invasions, diplomatic contact)? Historical medicine doesn't happen in a vacuum. Dynasty = context = exam answer.
"What did THIS figure/text CHANGE versus what came before? What problem were they solving?"
→ ⚖️ The Developmental Question — compare, contrast, trace evolution
Prof. Choi will ask you to compare: Nei Jing vs. later texts; Chinese medicine vs. Korean medicine development; classical theory vs. clinical evolution. The key is showing you understand DEVELOPMENT — medicine as a living tradition that responds to clinical realities, not a static text to memorize. What problem did Zhang Zhong Jing face? → epidemics. What problem did Ye Tian Shi face? → warm/febrile diseases different from Cold Damage.
"What is the significance for the KOREAN tradition? How did Korean medicine receive, adapt, or develop Chinese medicine independently?"
→ 🇰🇷 The Korean Lineage Question — Choi's specialty, expect exam depth here
Prof. Choi (Hyungsuk Choi, KMD) specializes in Korean medicine history. Dongui Bogam (허준 Heo Jun, 1613): Why commissioned? → Imjin War devastated medical infrastructure. What was innovative? → Korean herbs, not just imported Chinese — democratized medicine. Sasang Constitutional Medicine (이제마 Lee Je-ma, 1894): Why uniquely Korean? → Not derived from Chinese sources. What was revolutionary? → Same disease = different treatment by constitutional type. Know these cold.
"What does 'Naked History' mean? What is Prof. Choi's approach to medical history — and why does the stripping away of mythology matter?"
→ 📜 The Meta-Question — likely Day 1 and Exam 1 both
"Naked History" = stripping away mythology, hagiography, and official narrative to examine what actually happened — the political interests, power structures, practical realities of medicine. The texts attributed to legendary figures (Yellow Emperor, Shen Nong) were NOT written by those people. They were compiled by court scholars who used legendary attribution to give texts authority. Critical reading: who wrote these texts, who had economic incentives, what was suppressed, and the tension between canonical texts and folk medicine.
"How did the Bi Lei Qu Xiang method work? Why is analogical reasoning central to Eastern medicine's development?"
→ 🔮 The Reasoning Method Question — core methodology of AHM
Bi Lei Qu Xiang (比類取象 — analogical reasoning / "comparing categories and drawing from images") is the foundational reasoning method of Asian herbal medicine. Rather than Western causal/mechanistic reasoning, AHM reasons by analogy: a plant with red berries might treat Blood disorders; a root that grows downward might descend rebellious Qi. This is not superstition — it's a different epistemological framework. Understanding why this worked clinically over 2,000+ years is key to understanding the history of Eastern medicine.
"Why were there simultaneous abolition AND revival patterns for Eastern medicine in China, Korea, Japan, and Taiwan in the 20th century?"
→ 🏛️ The Modernization Question — 20th century geopolitics + medicine
In every East Asian country, the 20th century brought the same tension: Western-educated modernizers tried to abolish traditional medicine (it was "unscientific"), but popular demand and nationalist identity forced revivals. China: attempted abolition 1929 → revival under Mao as "Chinese medicine" 1950s → 1959 standardization (TCM). Korea: Japanese colonial suppression → post-independence revival. Japan: Koho-ha (classical) vs. Kokan Igaku Kampo (integrated Western) split. Taiwan: preserved classical tradition. The pattern shows medicine is not just science — it's politics and identity.
"What is the Mawangdui evidence and why does it matter for understanding the early history of meridian theory?"
→ 🔍 The Archaeological Evidence Question — empirical history of acupuncture
The Mawangdui texts (马王堆, discovered 1973, dated ~168 BCE) are the oldest medical manuscripts found in China. Critically: they describe approximately 11 channels (meridians) with NO acupoints — just channels. This predates the classical 12-channel + 365-acupoint system. This proves meridian theory developed gradually — it was NOT fully formed from the beginning. The Nei Jing (100 BCE) represents a later, more systematized stage. This archaeological evidence supports the "Naked History" approach: the classical system we use today was built up over centuries, not revealed whole.

⚔️ History of Eastern Medicine — Timeline

From legendary origins to modern standardization. Know figures + dates + WHY.
Chinese & Korean Medicine — Key Inflection Points

🐉 Chinese Medicine — Major Figures

c. 2700 BCE · Legendary
🐲 Yellow Emperor (黃帝 Huáng Dì) & Shen Nong (神農)
Legendary attribution — NOT real authors. Political move: court scholars used imperial names to give texts unquestionable authority. This is the "Naked History" point — unpack the mythology. Nei Jing attributed to Yellow Emperor + Qi Bo dialogue; Shen Nong Ben Cao attributed to Divine Farmer.
c. 168 BCE · Han Dynasty — MAWANGDUI
🔍 Mawangdui Medical Texts (马王堆)
Oldest medical manuscripts discovered (1973). Describe ~11 channels with NO acupoints — proof that the meridian system developed gradually. The classical 12-channel + 365-acupoint system came later. Key exam point: challenges the idea that the complete acupuncture system was "revealed" fully formed.
c. 100 BCE · Han Dynasty
📜 Huang Di Nei Jing (黃帝內經) — Yellow Emperor's Classic
Su Wen (素問 Basic Questions) — theory, cosmology, Yin/Yang, Five Phases, organ systems. Ling Shu (靈樞 Spiritual Pivot) — acupuncture channels, needling techniques. Compiled by court scholars, attributed to Yellow Emperor + Qi Bo. Foundation of all subsequent Eastern medicine theory.
c. 100 BCE · Han Dynasty
🌿 Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing (神農本草經)
365 medicinals classified into 3 grades: Superior (君 Jun — non-toxic, life-prolonging), Middle (臣 Chen — therapeutic), Inferior (佐 Zuo — toxic, specific conditions). Foundation of herbal medicine classification. Not written by Shen Nong — compiled during Han to systematize accumulated knowledge.
c. 150–219 CE · Han/Three Kingdoms
🏥 Zhang Zhong Jing (張仲景) — "Sage of Medicine"
Shang Han Lun (傷寒論 Treatise on Cold Damage) — Six Stages framework for epidemic febrile diseases. Context: devastating epidemics (possibly typhoid) killed 2/3 of his family. First CLINICAL manual — moved from theory to treatment protocols. Jinguei Yaolue (金匱要略) — internal medicine. The bridge from cosmological theory to clinical practice.
AD 281–341 · Jin Dynasty
☯️ Ge Hong (葛洪) — Daoist Physician
Zhou Hou Bei Ji Fang (肘後備急方 Emergency Formulas) — practical first aid for common people. Described smallpox, tuberculosis. Referenced 青蒿 (qinghao/artemisia) for fever — Tu Youyou later isolated artemisinin from it and won the 2015 Nobel Prize in Medicine. 1,700-year gap between classical reference and modern validation.
AD 215–282 · Jin Dynasty
💓 Wang Shu He (王叔和) — Pulse Classic
Mai Jing (脈經) — systematized 24 pulse qualities. Also edited and preserved Zhang Zhong Jing's Shang Han Lun — without Wang Shu He, this text might have been lost. Pulse diagnosis: not unique to China — appears in ancient Egyptian, Greek, and Indian traditions (Ayurvedic Nadi). Shows convergent development of clinical reasoning.
AD 581–682 · Tang Dynasty
👑 Sun Si Miao (孫思邈) — "King of Medicine" (藥王 Yào Wáng)
Bei Ji Qian Jin Yao Fang (備急千金要方 Prescriptions Worth 1,000 Gold). First Chinese medical ethics text — "great physician" must treat all equally regardless of social status. Sections on gynecology and pediatrics (previously neglected). Daoist longevity practices integrated with clinical medicine. Lived to approximately 100 years. Tang Dynasty = golden age of medicine.
AD 1115–1234 · Jin/Yuan Dynasties
⚖️ The Four Masters of Jin-Yuan (金元四大家)
Context: North China under Jin (Jurchen) rule — different climate, different diseases. Liu Wansu 劉完素 — Fire theory, cooling approach. Zhang Congzheng 張從正 — sweating/vomiting/purging, expel pathogenic Qi. Li Dongyuan 李東垣 — Spleen-Stomach theory, tonify Earth. Zhu Danxi 朱丹溪 — Yin deficiency is the root of all disease. Four distinct schools — shows medicine is not monolithic.
AD 1518–1593 · Ming Dynasty
🌿 Li Shi Zhen (李時珍) — Ben Cao Gang Mu (本草綱目)
Compendium of Materia Medica — 27 years to compile. 1,892 medicinals, 11,096 formulas, 1,110 illustrations. Greatest naturalist-physician in Chinese history. Corrected errors in previous materia medicas through direct observation. Translated into Latin, French, Russian — influenced global botany and natural history. Pi (脾) possibly mistranslated as "pancreas" by Western translators — functional organ, not anatomical.
AD 1798 · Qing Dynasty · Wang Qingren
🔬 Wang Qingren (王清任) — Anatomy & Blood Stasis
42 years of corpse dissection and observation to correct anatomical errors in classical texts. Yi Lin Gai Cuo (醫林改錯 Correcting Errors in the Forest of Medicine). Developed sophisticated Blood Stasis theory — 瘀血 (yū xuè). Parallel concept to Western 'thrombos' (Greek). Shows Eastern and Western medicine independently arrived at similar clinical observations through different frameworks.
AD 1667–1746 · Qing Dynasty
🔥 Ye Tian Shi (葉天士) — Warm Disease School
Wen Bing (溫病 Warm Disease) — Four Levels theory: Wei (衛 Defensive), Qi (氣 Functional), Ying (營 Nutritive), Xue (血 Blood). First Chinese physician to truly understand the infectious nature of certain epidemic diseases. Compare to Liu Qi/Liu Yin (Six Excesses/Six Pathogens) — analogous to modern host-pathogen theory, where the "host" environment determines vulnerability. Studied under 17 different teachers.
1929 → 1950 → 1959 · Republic of China → PRC
🏛️ Modernization — Abolition & Revival
1929: Chinese Nationalist government attempted abolition of traditional medicine — too "unscientific." Popular resistance forced reversal. 1950: Mao Zedong mandated integration of Chinese medicine into national healthcare — political and practical reasons. 1959: Full TCM standardization with Western scholars documenting: Manfred Porkert (Germany), Ted Kaptchuk (USA), Giovanni Maciocia (Italy). Simultaneously: Descartes/Flexner biomedical model critiqued for mind-body dualism.

🇰🇷 Korean Medicine — Prof. Choi's Focus

AD 1613 · Joseon Dynasty · After Imjin War
📚 Heo Jun (허준) — Dongui Bogam (동의보감)
Context: Japanese invasions (Imjin War, 1592–98) devastated Korean medical infrastructure — physicians killed, texts destroyed, supply chains collapsed. King Seonjo commissioned Heo Jun to compile comprehensive medical text. Key innovation: used KOREAN herbs instead of imported Chinese → democratized medicine for common people who couldn't afford imports. 25 volumes. UNESCO Memory of the World 2009. Still referenced clinically. Heo Jun = "the Zhang Zhong Jing of Korea."
AD 1894 · Late Joseon Dynasty
⚖️ Lee Je-ma (이제마) — Sasang Constitutional Medicine (사상의학)
Dongui Susebowon (동의수세보원). UNIQUELY Korean — NOT derived from Chinese sources. Four constitutional types based on relative organ strength: Taeyang (太陽, very rare, strong Lung/weak Liver), Taeeum (太陰, most common ~50%), Soyang (少陽, ~30%), Soeum (少陰, ~20%). Same disease = different treatment by constitutional type. Revolutionary individualization of medicine — predating modern personalized medicine by 130 years.
20th–21st Century
🏫 Korean Oriental Medicine (KOM) — Modernization & Independence
Japanese colonial period: suppression of Korean traditional medicine. Post-independence: revival and institutionalization. Kyung Hee University — first Oriental Medicine university in Korea (1965). Now: Korean medicine distinct legally and clinically from Chinese medicine within Korea. KOM practitioners (KMD — Korean Medical Doctor) hold separate licenses. Korean government integrates KOM into national healthcare. Prof. Choi (KMD) represents this tradition.

🌪️ Liu Qi / Liu Yin — Six Pathogenic Qi

🔬
Host-Pathogen Theory Connection Liu Qi/Liu Yin (Six Excesses/Six Yin pathogens) = Wind, Cold, Summer-Heat, Dampness, Dryness, Fire. These external pathogens cause disease when host defenses (Wei Qi) are insufficient. Analogous to modern host-pathogen theory where susceptibility depends on host immune status, not just pathogen exposure. Max von Pettenkofer's 1892 cholera self-experiment — same parallel.

🌿 Landmark Formulas to Know

Gui Zhi Tang
桂枝湯
Shang Han Lun's first formula. Tai Yang exterior deficiency. Cinnamon twig, white peony, ginger, jujube, licorice. The foundational building block of classical formulation.
Ma Huang Tang
麻黃湯
Tai Yang exterior excess (no sweating). Ma huang, gui zhi, almond, licorice. The archetypal cold-expelling formula of the Shang Han tradition.
Si Jun Zi Tang
四君子湯
Four Gentlemen — foundation of Spleen Qi tonification. Ren shen, bai zhu, fu ling, zhi gan cao. Base of Li Dongyuan's Spleen-Stomach school.
Yin Qiao San
銀翹散
Warm Disease school — Wei level Heat. Honeysuckle + forsythia + mint. Ye Tian Shi's approach: cool-pungent, not warm-pungent. KEY differentiation from Shang Han approach.
