Prologue & Appendix

Why We Learn History · Reference & Chinese Characters
0 / 22 prepared

What This Chapter Covers

The book opens with something more personal than a typical syllabus: the author explains that his own love of history came from his father, a history teacher who told stories rather than drilling dates, and who passed away from cancer during the author's own studies — the book is written, in part, to pass that same storytelling instinct on. From there it turns practical: for English-speaking or Western-trained students, the historical and philosophical gap can't be closed by memorizing terms alone, any more than you could understand Shakespeare well without the historical context around him. The Prologue also raises a harder identity question worth sitting with all quarter: what actually makes something "acupuncture and herbal medicine"? Is a doctor using licorice or cinnamon twig inside a Western biomedical framework still practicing AHM — and if identity depends on more than the tools used, history is the only way to find out. That same identity question is not just a Week 1 throat-clear — the book deliberately circles back to it at the very end of Chapter 4, and again as the explicit thesis of Chapter 6. It's the spine of the entire book.

  • The author's personal motivation for teaching history — passed down from his own father
  • Why history is not optional context but foundational to identity as a practitioner
  • The Western-student vs. Asian-student knowledge gap, and the Shakespeare analogy
  • "Understanding the Identity of AHM through History" — what actually makes something AHM
  • "Naked history" as a method — stripping myth from verified fact
  • Course learning outcomes CO1–CO4 and what each will actually be tested on
  • The identity question this opens with is revisited at the close of Chapter 4 and as Chapter 6's explicit thesis

Walk Into Class Able To Explain

  • Frames students as a living link in an unbroken clinical lineage
  • Sets up the course's central tension, developed later: "Worship or Ignore?"
  • Chinese character appendix is reference material — return to it weekly
  • History-first sequencing is a deliberate pedagogical choice, not filler

Chapter 1 — History of AHM

Mythos to Logos · Three Sovereigns & Five Emperors · Pan Gu · Huang Di · Bian Que
0 / 22 prepared

What This Chapter Covers

Chapter 1 ("History of AHM") opens by defining AHM's identity, then walks the mythos-to-logos shift: Fuxi, Nuwa, and Shennong as cultural-hero myths from the Longshan Neolithic period, through the Xia/Shang/Zhou bronze-age states, into the Spring & Autumn and Warring States periods and the "Hundred Schools of Thought." From there the book marches dynasty by dynasty — Qin, Han, Sui-Tang, Song, Yuan, Ming, Qing — and for each one gives a short narrative, an "In the World" section comparing what was happening elsewhere on the planet at the same time, and a Key Characteristics / Key Publications summary. The book leans hard on pop-culture hooks to anchor each era (The Mummy for Qin Shi Huang, Journey to the West and Dragon Ball for Tang-dynasty Buddhism and the Silk Road, Marco Polo for the Yuan, Ip Man for Qing-era foreign conflict) and closes the chapter with an explicit mnemonic for the dynasty sequence.

  • Definition and historical origins of AHM — where the "identity" question from the Prologue gets answered
  • Fuxi, Nuwa, Shennong, and the Longshan Neolithic culture
  • Xia, Shang, Zhou dynasties and the Spring & Autumn / Warring States periods
  • Qin Shi Huang's unification and the short, harsh Qin Dynasty
  • Han Dynasty: Huang Di Nei Jing, Shang Han Lun, Shen Nong Ben Cao, Jia Yi Jing, Mai Jing
  • Sui–Tang, Song, Yuan, Ming, Qing — each dynasty's medical "Key Publications"
  • The dynasty mnemonic: "Happy Sloths Travel Slowly, Yearning Meditative Quietness" (Han–Sui–Tang–Song–Yuan–Ming–Qing)
  • Pop-culture anchors the book itself uses per dynasty (The Mummy, Journey to the West, Marco Polo, Ip Man)

Walk Into Class Able To Explain

  • Mythos → logos: legendary founders give way to systematic theory
  • Three Sovereigns & Five Emperors as an attribution strategy, not verified history
  • Bian Que may represent a title passed across generations of itinerant physicians
  • Warring States instability as fertile ground for new philosophy and medicine

Chapter 2 — Upon a Time in China: Philosophical Background

Yin-Yang · Five Phases (the "five departments" analogy) · Daoism, Confucianism, Legalism
0 / 22 prepared

What This Chapter Covers

Titled "Upon a Time in China: Philosophical Background" in the book itself, and it runs longer than its two assigned weeks suggest — deep into what a syllabus-level view would call Chapter 3 territory. Yin-yang is presented as relative and mutually transforming, never fixed. The Five Phases (Wu Xing) get an especially clear teaching device: imagine dividing a company into five departments — what matters isn't the department's name but its function and relationships to the others. The book is explicit that its own correspondence tables (organs/tastes/pathology) are "flexible tools rather than fixed entities," not absolute law. It also covers Liu Qi and Liu Yin (the Six Qi and Six Excesses/pathogenic influences), drawing a direct, sourced comparison to the modern "host-pathogen relationship" debate in microbiology — citing real research on the West African Ebola epidemic. And it names the underlying logical method AHM itself runs on: Bi Lei Qu Xiang, "obtaining an image by comparing types" — analogical reasoning, the same kind of thinking that let engineers managing the Yellow River's waterways become a model for meridian theory.

  • Yin-Yang theory: relativity, interdependence, and mutual transformation
  • The Four Movements of Yin-Yang mapped onto the Five Phases (Wu Xing)
  • The "five departments in a company" analogy for understanding Wu Xing as function, not substance
  • The book's own caveat: Five Element correspondence tables are "flexible tools," not fixed entities
  • Liu Qi and Liu Yin (Six Qi / Six Excesses) compared directly to the modern host-pathogen debate
  • Bi Lei Qu Xiang — analogical reasoning as AHM's core epistemological method
  • Daoist philosophy: the Dao, wu wei, and natural self-regulating order
  • Confucian philosophy: hierarchy, duty, and correct relationships applied to the body
  • Legalist philosophy: codified law and centralized control — EAM's faintest influence
  • CO2 directly: comparing and contrasting all three schools' medical contributions

📊 The Five Phases (Wu Xing) — Sheng & Ke Cycles

Green = Sheng (generating) cycle — Wood feeds Fire, Fire feeds Earth, and so on. Red = Ke (controlling) cycle — Wood parts Earth, Earth dams Water, and so on. Both cycles run simultaneously; that's the whole point.

Walk Into Class Able To Explain

  • Yin-yang: relative, interdependent, mutually transforming — never fixed categories
  • Five Phases: generating (Sheng) and controlling (Ke) cycles work together
  • Daoism → natural order, wu wei; Confucianism → hierarchy, duty; Legalism → codified control
  • Philosophy precedes clinical text — theory came before the Nei Jing and Shang Han Lun

Chapter 3 — Cornerstones of AHM

Nei Jing · Shen Nong Ben Cao · Shang Han Lun · Hua Tuo · Sun Si Miao · Early Acupuncture
0 / 22 prepared

What This Chapter Covers

The clinical foundation chapter — where philosophy turns into text you could actually practice from. It doesn't just present acupuncture channel theory as a finished system; it shows the archaeological evidence for how gradually it actually formed. Early excavated medical texts (the Mawangdui-era literature catalogued by scholars like Donald Harper) describe only moxibustion, not needling; they name meridian lines but no actual acupoints; they describe roughly eleven meridians rather than the twelve used today; and only two or three of those meridians were even connected to internal organs. The now-familiar unified, organ-linked, twelve-channel circulatory system was not a circular system in its early days — it was assembled over centuries. The book also gives pulse diagnosis a genuinely global history — ancient Egyptian physicians were palpating and timing the pulse with a water-clock as early as the Ebers Papyrus, alongside independent traditions in Greek, Persian, and Ayurvedic medicine — and the author adds his own opinionated aside that "pulse diagnosis is not omnipotent, [but] it is useful," pushing back on the idea that a skilled pulse-reader can diagnose pregnancy or cancer by feel alone. A close look at the word "Lun" (treatise) versus "Jing" (canon) in text titles shows that some of AHM's most influential works — including the Shang Han Lun itself — never claimed scriptural authority to begin with. And the chapter includes a genuinely startling figure: Wang Qingren, a Qing-dynasty physician who spent 42 years dissecting executed prisoners' and abandoned corpses to correct anatomical errors in classical texts — enduring ridicule from colleagues who preferred inherited theory over direct observation, in a story the book explicitly likens to Vesalius correcting Galen in the West.

  • Huang Di Nei Jing: Su Wen (theory) and Ling Shu (acupuncture technique)
  • Archaeological evidence for how channel theory actually formed — moxibustion before needling, no acupoints yet, only ~11 meridians
  • Why the unified twelve-channel, organ-linked system was a gradual construction, not an original design
  • Zhang Zhong Jing, the Shang Han Lun, and the Six Stages framework
  • "Lun" (treatise) vs. "Jing" (canon) — what a text's title implies about its claimed authority
  • A global history of pulse diagnosis: ancient Egypt's water-clock method, Greek, Persian, and Ayurvedic parallels
  • The author's own take: pulse diagnosis is useful but not omnipotent — a corrective to over-mystification
  • Wang Qingren — 42 years of corpse dissection to correct anatomical errors, and the book's direct Vesalius comparison
  • The Jin-Yuan Four Great Masters, including Zhu Danxi (Nourishing Yin) and Li Gao (Spleen-Stomach)
  • Direct comparison to Japan's Kohoha school of Kampo and Yoshimasu Todo
  • Korean dry-needling technique described in the Chimgugyeongheombang, cross-checked against modern ultrasound needle-depth research
  • Sun Si Miao's medical ethics, gynecology, and pediatrics

Walk Into Class Able To Explain

  • Nei Jing is two compiled texts, not one author, one era
  • Zhang Zhong Jing's Six Stages theory emerged directly from epidemic tragedy
  • Shen Nong Ben Cao's three-grade system prioritizes long-term safety, not just efficacy
  • Wang Shu He's editorial work is why Shang Han Lun survived at all

Chapter 4 — Now and Future

TCM · Modernization of AHM · Worship or Ignore? · Integrated Medicine · Acupuncture Research
0 / 22 prepared

What This Chapter Covers

The chapter pivots hard into the present tense — and gives a specific, citable anchor most students never learn: in 1959, China formally decided to standardize a national curriculum for what would become "Traditional Chinese Medicine," fusing 15 subjects that blended classical theory (Nei Jing, Shang Han Lun, Wen Bing, case studies) with modern clinical specialties (internal medicine, surgery, gynecology, pediatrics, ophthalmology). That standardized curriculum is also explicitly what carried AHM to the West: the book names Manfred Porkert, Ted Kaptchuk, and Giovanni Maciocia as the Western scholars whose books introduced systematic AHM theory to English-speaking audiences. A striking research timeline shows China, Korea, Japan, and Taiwan all abolished traditional medicine around the same early-20th-century window — and all later revived it, with Japanese physician Wada Keijuro's 1915 book comparing Kampo to Western medicine credited as an early spark of that revival. Choi frames the modern dilemma as "Worship or Ignore?" and the chapter's final page closes the loop the Prologue opened: the author, an AHM doctor practicing in California, reflects that whether you call yourself an AHM doctor or an apothecary, you're doing essentially the same work as physicians of the past — and that in South Korea, "Western doctor" and "AHM doctor" are still used side by side, with equal legal standing.

  • 1959: the specific year China standardized the national "TCM" curriculum — 15 fused traditional + modern subjects
  • "TCM" as a 20th-century standardized institutional construction, not an ancient monolith
  • Named Western scholars who brought AHM to the U.S.: Manfred Porkert, Ted Kaptchuk, Giovanni Maciocia

Chapter 5 — Besides China

What This Chapter Covers

Most EAM history survey courses are quietly China-centered, with everything else reduced to a footnote — this chapter is the correction, and it's very likely where Prof. Choi's own training and specialty (he's a Korean Medicine Doctor) shows up most heavily on your exams. Heo Jun's Dongui Bogam emerges directly from crisis: after the Imjin War devastated Korea's medical infrastructure and cut off imported Chinese materials, he compiled a synthesis built on domestic Korean herbs — genuinely democratizing medicine. Three centuries later, Lee Je-ma's Sasang Constitutional Medicine breaks even further from Chinese theoretical assumptions, arguing treatment should vary by constitutional type even for identical symptoms. Japan's Kampo tradition isn't treated as one thing, either — the book splits it into two competing schools: Yoshimasu Todo's 18th-century Koho-ha, which rejected abstract yin-yang and Five Phase theory in favor of a pragmatic "one poison" approach to disease; and the early-20th-century Kokan Igaku of Yumoto Kyushin, which tried to re-verify Kampo's old formulas using Western pathology and physiology. Even the name "Kokan" carries nationalist symbolism — combining the character for the Japanese Empire with the character for the Han dynasty of China.

What This Chapter Covers

The book states this chapter's thesis outright on its title page: modernity has left "the West" and "the East" in constant, ongoing tension, and comparing them directly is meant to give you "a clearer identity of Acupuncture and Herbal Medicine" — the same identity question the Prologue opened with. The Qing dynasty brings two disruptions at once: an internal theoretical breakthrough (Wen Bing Tiao Bian, the systematic differentiation of warm diseases) and an external cultural collision with Western biomedicine, anchored in the true story of Max von Pettenkofer drinking live cholera bacteria in 1892 to defend Miasma theory against Koch's Germ Theory — and surviving, largely by luck. But the chapter's sharpest point may be a translation problem hiding in plain sight: the AHM organ "Pi" (脾), conventionally translated as "Spleen," functionally aligns far more closely with the modern pancreas — blood sugar regulation, digestive enzyme secretion — than with the anatomical spleen's actual job (filtering blood, immune response). The book poses the question directly: if a patient had an anatomical spleen problem, would you treat their Pi? It closes with a similar cross-civilization pairing — Greek "thrombos" and Chinese 瘀血 (yūxuè, stagnant blood) independently arriving at strikingly similar concepts — and a critique of Evidence-Based Medicine's own history, from Descartes' clockwork-body metaphor through the Flexner Report, arguing that "absence of evidence is not evidence of absence."

Yellow Emperor 黄帝 (Huáng Dì)
Mythological progenitor. Dialogue with Qi Bo frames the Nei Jing. Not a historical figure — an attribution strategy lending authority to later compilations.
c. 100 BCE · Han Dynasty
Huang Di Nei Jing 黄帝内经
Su Wen (theory, cosmology, organ physiology) + Ling Shu (acupuncture technique, channels). Compiled over centuries, not written by one author.
c. 100 BCE · Han Dynasty
Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing 神农本草经
365 medicinals in 3 grades: Superior (tonify/longevity), Middle (treat without harm), Lower (treat disease, side effects possible).
AD 150–219 · Han / Three Kingdoms
Zhang Zhong Jing 张仲景
Shang Han Za Bing Lun → later split into Shang Han Lun + Jin Gui Yao Lue. Six Stages theory. Written after an epidemic killed two-thirds of his clan.
AD 215–282
Wang Shu He 王叔和 — Mai Jing 脉经
First systematic pulse text — 24 pulse qualities. Preserved and edited Zhang Zhong Jing's work; without him it may have been lost.
AD 281–341 · Jin Dynasty
Ge Hong 葛洪
Zhou Hou Bei Ji Fang — first-aid manual for common people. Referenced qinghao (sweet wormwood) for fever; 1700 years later, source of Tu Youyou's Nobel-winning artemisinin.
AD 581–682 · Tang Dynasty
Sun Si Miao 孙思邈 — "King of Medicine"
Bei Ji Qian Jin Yao Fang. First Chinese medical ethics text. Gynecology, pediatrics, dietetics. Daoist longevity practice integrated with clinical medicine.
AD 1518–1593 · Ming Dynasty
Li Shi Zhen 李时珍
Ben Cao Gang Mu — 27 years to compile, 1,892 medicinals, 1,110 illustrations. Translated into multiple languages; influenced global botany.
AD 1667–1746 · Qing Dynasty
Ye Tian Shi 叶天士
Wen Bing (Warm Disease) school. Four Levels theory (Wei/Qi/Ying/Xue). First to systematically distinguish febrile epidemic disease from cold-induced disease.
AD 1613 · Joseon Dynasty
Heo Jun 허준 — Dongui Bogam 동의보감
Commissioned by King Seonjo after the Imjin War. Used Korean herbs instead of imported Chinese ones — democratized medicine. UNESCO Memory of the World, 2009.
AD 1894 · Joseon Dynasty
Lee Je-ma 이제마 — Sasang Medicine
Dongui Susebowon. Four constitutional types: Taeyang, Taeeum, Soyang, Soeum. Same disease, different treatment by constitution.
20th–21st c.
Korean Oriental Medicine (KOM)
Kyung Hee University — first Oriental Medicine university in Korea (1965). Legally and clinically distinct from Chinese medicine today.
